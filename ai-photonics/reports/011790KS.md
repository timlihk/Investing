### Thesis (2-3 sentences)
SKC's subsidiary Absolics is the world's first company to mass-produce glass core substrates for advanced semiconductor packaging, including co-packaged optics (CPO). Glass substrates offer superior electrical performance, thermal stability, and ultra-fine through-glass vias (TGVs) versus organic substrates -- critical enablers for next-gen AI packaging where photonic and electronic chips are co-located. However, SKC the parent is a deeply unprofitable Korean conglomerate (W734B net loss in 2025, negative gross margins, 185% debt-to-equity) burning cash across legacy businesses, making this a high-risk, pre-revenue venture capital bet wrapped inside a troubled industrial company.

### CPO Relevance
Glass substrates are relevant to CPO but not exclusively so. The connection:
- CPO requires co-packaging photonic ICs (lasers, modulators, photodetectors) with electronic ICs (switches, SerDes) on a single substrate. This demands substrates with ultra-low warpage, tight dimensional stability at high temperatures, and fine-pitch interconnects.
- Glass substrates excel here: coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) closer to silicon than organic substrates, enabling larger panel sizes with less warpage. Through-glass vias (TGVs) can be finer-pitch than organic vias.
- However, CPO is also being developed on organic substrates (Ibiden, Shinko) and silicon interposers (TSMC CoWoS). Glass is a potential upgrade path, not the only path.
- The stronger near-term use case for glass substrates is 2.5D/3D chiplet packaging for AI accelerators (HBM integration, multi-die GPU/TPU packages) -- which is adjacent to but broader than CPO.

**Verdict on CPO link**: Glass substrates are a CPO enabler, not a CPO necessity. The AI advanced packaging TAM is the real driver.

### Moat Analysis
**Rating: Weak (2/5)**

The moat story is aspirational rather than demonstrated:
- **First-mover advantage**: Absolics is first to mass production of glass core substrates. This matters but is not durable -- Samsung, AGC, and others are 1-2 years behind, not decades.
- **CHIPS Act funding**: $75M + $100M in grants provides a cost advantage for the Covington facility, but doesn't prevent competitors from building elsewhere.
- **Applied Materials investment**: Signals validation but $40M is small relative to the capex required.
- **No revenue moat**: Without disclosed customer commitments or design wins generating revenue, the moat is theoretical.
- **Parent company drag**: SKC's legacy businesses have negative gross margins and massive debt. The conglomerate structure means Absolics value is diluted by parent-level losses.

### Catalysts
1. **Absolics mass production confirmation** (H1 2026): If Absolics begins shipping glass substrates to NVIDIA/AMD in volume, this would be a major catalyst. Currently unconfirmed.
2. **Customer design win disclosure**: A named customer qualification (e.g., NVIDIA Blackwell Ultra or next-gen packaging) would transform the thesis from speculative to credible.
3. **Glass substrate market inflection**: Market projected to grow from $195M (2024) to $572M by 2031 (17% CAGR). The inflection point for Absolics revenue would be 2027-2028.
4. **Absolics spinoff/IPO**: If SKC separates Absolics, the substrate business could be valued independently without the drag of legacy chemicals. No timeline announced.
5. **CHIPS Act milestone payments**: Continued drawdowns from the $75M incentive would confirm facility progress.

### Risks
1. **Pre-revenue subsidiary inside a loss-making conglomerate**: SKC lost KRW 734B in FY2025 with negative gross margins. The entire investment thesis depends on an unproven subsidiary. The parent is burning ~KRW 857B in FCF annually.
2. **Massive debt load**: KRW 3.74T in total debt (185% D/E) with negative operating cash flow. The Feb 2026 equity raise of KRW 1T provides runway but also diluted existing shareholders.
3. **Competitor catch-up**: Samsung Electro-Mechanics, AGC, Nippon Electric Glass, and potentially Intel are all developing glass substrates. First-mover advantage may last only 1-2 years, not a structural moat.
4. **Technology risk**: Organic interposers and silicon interposers (TSMC CoWoS) are proven, shipping at volume. Glass substrates must demonstrate superior yield and reliability in production -- not just in the lab.
5. **Korean conglomerate discount**: SKC is part of the SK Group chaebol. Related-party transactions, opaque capital allocation, and minority shareholder interests are structural concerns.
6. **Geopolitical exposure**: While Absolics is building in the US (Covington, GA), SKC parent operations are Korea-based. US-China tensions could affect customer relationships.
7. **No disclosed Absolics financials**: Investors cannot independently verify Absolics revenue, margins, or path to profitability. This is a black box inside a troubled parent.

**Sum-of-parts approach:**
- **Legacy businesses** (chemicals, copper foil): Given negative margins and declining fundamentals, legacy businesses contribute near-zero or negative equity value. Generously: KRW 500B-1T based on book value of tangible assets minus debt attributable to legacy.

At 50% market share: $800M-$1.5B.
- **Sum**: KRW 1.2T-2.4T in a base case, vs KRW 3.87T market cap. The market is pricing in a very optimistic Absolics scenario.

**Implied Absolics valuation**: Stripping out legacy value (near zero), the market is assigning ~$2.5B+ to Absolics alone -- roughly 4-5x the projected 2031 glass substrate TAM. This prices in dominant market share AND premium valuation multiples.

At current prices, SKC is overvalued relative to fundamental evidence. The market has already priced in the Absolics story.

### Verdict: AVOID
SKC is the highest-risk name in the AI photonics universe. The Absolics glass substrate story is genuinely interesting -- first-to-market in a critical enabling technology for advanced AI packaging and potentially CPO. But the investment vehicle is toxic: a loss-making Korean conglomerate with negative gross margins, KRW 3.74T in debt, burning KRW 857B in annual FCF, with no disclosed Absolics financials. The market is already pricing in an optimistic Absolics outcome at KRW 3.87T market cap. Competitors (Samsung, AGC, Nippon Electric Glass) are 1-2 years behind, not decades. The correct way to play the glass substrate thesis is to wait for either (a) Absolics to be spun off/IPO'd as a standalone entity, (b) disclosed customer design wins with revenue, or (c) a significant pullback in SKC shares that creates margin of safety. None of those conditions exist today.

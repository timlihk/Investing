# JBL - Jabil Inc.
## Date: 2026-04-12
## Score: 55/100

### Thesis (2-3 sentences)
Jabil is a $33B revenue contract electronics manufacturer that acquired Intel's silicon photonics pluggable transceiver module business in 2023, making it a direct competitor to Fabrinet (FN) in 800G/1.6T optical transceiver assembly. With a $500M North Carolina plant for data center photonics and a March 2026 collaboration with HyperLight and UMC on thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN) manufacturing, Jabil is positioning itself as a credible second source for AI photonics contract manufacturing. However, at $34B total revenue, photonics is <5% of the business -- making this an "ocean with a photonics island" rather than a targeted chokepoint play.

### Moat Analysis
**Rating: Moderate (3/5)**

Jabil's photonics moat is real but diluted by its diversified nature:
- **Intel SiPh asset acquisition**: Jabil now owns the production capability for Intel's silicon photonics pluggable transceivers. This is genuine manufacturing IP -- not just assembly, but full module integration using Intel SiPh engines.
- **$500M NC plant**: Purpose-built for data center photonics, signaling long-term commitment. But this is a single plant in a $34B company.
- **TFLN collaboration (HyperLight/UMC)**: Positions Jabil on the next-generation photonics platform (thin-film lithium niobate). This is forward-looking and differentiating.
- **Scale advantage**: 135,000 employees, global manufacturing footprint. Can offer hyperscalers redundancy vs Thailand-concentrated Fabrinet.
- **Weakness -- 9% gross margins**: This is contract manufacturing economics. Fabrinet achieves 10-12% gross margins on optical alone. Jabil's blended margins are even lower because non-photonics business (automotive, healthcare, industrial) is commodity CM work.
- **Weakness -- diversification as dilution**: Photonics is <5% of revenue. Management attention, capital allocation, and investor narrative are split across healthcare, automotive, industrial, defense, and data center.
- **Weakness -- not the incumbent**: Fabrinet has 15+ years of optical transceiver manufacturing relationships. Jabil is the challenger with 2-3 years of SiPh module experience.

### Chokepoint Assessment
Jabil is NOT a chokepoint in the same way Fabrinet is. Key differences:
1. **Fabrinet**: ~50% of all AI optical transceivers flow through one Thai campus. If Fabrinet stops, the industry halts.
2. **Jabil**: Provides an alternative source. If Jabil stops photonics, customers shift more to Fabrinet (capacity constraint, not supply chain break).
3. **Role**: Jabil is a chokepoint *loosener* -- it provides the industry with a second source, reducing Fabrinet's monopoly power.
4. **TFLN angle**: If TFLN becomes the dominant platform post-SiPh, Jabil/HyperLight/UMC could become a chokepoint for that specific technology. But this is 3-5 years out and speculative.

### Catalysts
1. **$500M NC photonics plant completion**: Creates US-based capacity for security-sensitive hyperscaler demand.
2. **1.6T ramp**: As 1.6T transceivers ramp in 2026-2027, Jabil's Intel SiPh-based modules are well-positioned.
3. **TFLN manufacturing**: If HyperLight TFLN achieves commercial traction, Jabil is the manufacturing partner.
4. **Hyperscaler diversification**: Cloud companies wanting a second source beyond Fabrinet may accelerate Jabil qualification.
5. **FCF generation**: $1.2B FCF supports continued investment in photonics without dilution.

### Risks
1. **Photonics is immaterial to financials**: Even a 50% increase in photonics revenue wouldn't meaningfully move the needle on a $33B revenue base.
2. **9% gross margins**: Contract manufacturing economics limit upside. Jabil doesn't capture the value-add that component companies (MACOM, Broadcom) enjoy.
3. **Execution risk**: Integrating Intel's SiPh business and ramping a new NC plant while managing a 135K-employee global operation is complex.
4. **Fabrinet's entrenched relationships**: Switching optical CM providers requires 6-12 month qualification. Fabrinet's customer stickiness is formidable.
5. **Valuation at 40x trailing P/E**: Stock is near all-time highs. Expectations are high across all business segments, not just photonics.

### Valuation
At 0.97x P/S, Jabil looks cheap -- but this reflects CM economics (9% gross margins). The forward P/E of 21x is reasonable for a company growing 23%, but the growth is broad-based, not photonics-driven. As a photonics investment, Jabil offers indirect exposure diluted by $30B+ of non-photonics revenue. Fabrinet (FN) at 20x forward P/E with 10-12% gross margins and 90%+ optical revenue is a purer play. Jabil is a good company but an unfocused photonics vehicle.

### Verdict: HOLD
Jabil is a credible and growing participant in AI photonics contract manufacturing, but it fails the chokepoint test. Photonics is <5% of revenue, gross margins are commodity-grade at 9%, and the company is a second source to Fabrinet rather than an irreplaceable supplier. The Intel SiPh acquisition and TFLN collaboration are strategically interesting but won't drive the stock. Buy Jabil if you want diversified CM exposure with a photonics option; buy Fabrinet if you want the photonics chokepoint.

### Price Target: $320 (7% upside)
Based on FY26E EPS of ~$14.50 x 22x P/E = $319. Photonics optionality provides modest multiple expansion potential.
